Quotes:

When you’ve run out of ideas or passion for what you are working on: You should give a customer or a problem that you care about two years. You should work two years on something before it’s working. If you’re not willing to work two years on something you probably shouldn’t work on it at all.
A lot of people go into a startup thinking they know all the answers but when you ask startup founders, they never know the answers when they start. The first two years is when you’re becoming an expert and it’s very hard to skip that.
It’s very easy for you (as a startup founder) to believe that the vision in your head is the right thing but it’s almost never right and you will only find the right thing by putting something in front of users and seeing how they use it. You’re always surprised when you do that and you’ll always learn something.
I had to start understanding how important a team is versus an idea. Definitely a team that has some personal relationship. Definitely a team that has technical talent, but it doesn’t have to be very experienced. Definitely a team that has passion about the problem it’s solving. Definitely a team that is more excited about arriving at the right answer than being right.
The first thing I look for is a technical team of founders who can build the product or at least a first version of the product themselves. The second thing I look for is team of folks who know each other, who have worked with each other in some way or are friends with each other, who are going to be able to survive all the challenges of doing a startup together. The third thing I look for is how much progress the founding team is making over time. If the company has been around for three months, am I impressed by the progress they had made in this time?
One of YC’s (Y-Combinator) core thesis is that software is still very early in it’s adoption curve in the world.
A lot of what founders do is make mistakes because they act out of fear. When founders are afraid, they tend to run away from the tasks that they should be running towards. “I’m afraid my users won’t like my product. So instead of talking to them, I won’t talk to them.” “I’m afraid that customers don’t want to buy the product. So instead of trying to sell it to them, I don’t sell it at all.” “I’m afraid that this employee is going to get angry when I have to fire them because they are not doing their job well and so I won’t fire them.”
Fear is a great signal, because it tells them what to run towards. Another way to say this: Look at what’s on your to-do list and whatever makes you uncomfortable, do those things first.
YC’s three key pieces of advice: 1. Make something that people want. 2. Do something that doesn’t scale. 3. Talk to you user. → Description of advice number 2.: You’re an underdog. Almost always you have a big competitor and almost always you have to fight asymmetrically. You can’t copy the big player to beat them. You’ve got to do something differently than them. So, to do things that don’t scale means that you have to do something that your competitor can’t do. Example: My competitor cannot have a great customer service because a 100,000 people call them every hour. But only four of our customers call every hour, so we can have amazing customer service. → Will we be able to do the same customer service when we get big? - Probably not. But when we are trying to figure out how to make an amazing product we want our customers to know that we love then and we want to learn as much as possible from them.
Power Law Rule: Your best investments will make far more money than any of the rest.
Don’t assume how big the winners (companies that succeed) can get. They can get way bigger than you might think. Don’t so much worry about what would happen when the company lost, think a lot about how big could the company get if it won.
