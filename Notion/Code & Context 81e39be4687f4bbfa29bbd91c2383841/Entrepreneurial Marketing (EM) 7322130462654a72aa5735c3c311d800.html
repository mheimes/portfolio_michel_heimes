(Developing your) Marketing Strategy
Segmenting your customers
Breaking customers down into well-defined groups helps you focus only on the most relevant customers and avoids wasting time and money on the less relevant.
  1. Demographic segmentation → customers grouped by their characteristics, such as income level, age, gender or their height and weight. → Useful for products tied to these special traits.
  1. Geographic segmentation → Physical location of customers → Where does opening stores and advertising the products/ services make sense.
  1. Behavioral segmentation → Grouping customers by the things they do (hobbies and habits) → Also: How much do they purchase? How frequently do they purchase? What is their price sensitivity?
  1. Psychographic segmentation → Customer thought process (how they think) → Attitudes and aspirations (especially about the benefits that a product or service delivers) → Examples: Need for prestige, Need for convenience (Bequemlichkeit)

All four types of Customer Segmentation should be used!

  • Demographic and geographic segmentation help quantify the market potential and find the market areas that have the most opportunity.
  • Behavioral segmentation helps predicting sales and shopping behavior.
  • Psychographic segmentation helps define and create value proposition → What you say to the market to attract and retain customers
Targeting your Customers
Process of narrowing down your audience to a selected group to find the largest group of customers that are most willing to buying your products or services based on your marketing appeal.
Positioning your message

USP (Unique Selling Proposition/ Point) → Examples: lowest costs, highest quality, most experience, first in it’s product class or another trait that sets the offering apart from the competition. → What you have what the competitors don’t have.

  • USP should be/ have: 1. Relevance → Should address a key success factor in the market. 2. Visibility → Has to be visible to the potential customers. 3. Sustainability (haltbar) → A company has to be able to maintain this advantage.

Kano Modell zur Analyse Kundenzufriedenheit-Produkteigenschaften

  • Basisanforderungen = Hygienefaktoren → Must haves! → Sind sie nicht oder nur schlecht erfüllt, so sinkt die Kundenzufriedenheit überproportional! → Sind sie vollkommen erfüllt, wirkt sich dies eher neutral auf die Kundenzufriedenheit aus.
  • Qualitäts-/ Leistungsanforderungen → Werden von Kunden explizit erwartet. → Haben entsprechend der Features einen direkten und linearen Einfluss auf die Kundenzufriedenheit. → Viele Kunden vergleichen diese Merkmale/ Faktoren mit denen anderen Unternehmen.
  • Begeisterungs-/ Leistungsanforderungen → Sind keine must-have-Features aber begeistern und überzeugen zum Kauf! → Sind temporär und werden nach und nach zur Normalität. Entsprechend kann man sich nicht auf Begeisterungsfaktoren/ -merkmalen ausruhen! → Sind sie stark erfüllt, so steigt die Kundenzufriedenheit überproportional! → Sind sie nicht oder nur schlecht erfüllt, wirkt sich dies eher neutral auf die Kundenzufriedenheit aus.
  • Indifferenzzone → In diesem Bereich verhalten sich die Zufriedenheitswerte moderat, außerhalb dieser Zone steigen und sinken die Zufriedenheitswerte überproportional.

Value Proposition (Wertversprechen) → A single-minded (zielstrebig) claim to change the customer’s mind and cause them to do something.

How to define the Value Proposition - Five Box Positioning Tool:

  • Define current Do → What are customers doing today with respect to your product or service category?
  • Define current Belief → What are customers’ current beliefs about the products and services out in the market today?
  • Desired Do → What do You want customers to do?
  • Desired Beliefs → What beliefs will lead to the “Desired Do”?
  • Making a Claim → What claim must you make? → What supporting evidence do you have

Example:

  1. Current Do: “I use a traditional wallet, and it's thick because of all the stuff I carry.”
  1. Current Belief: “I believe my wallet sticks out a lot, but all wallets are the same.”
  1. Desired Do: “I buy a slim wallet.”
  1. Desired Belief: “I believe having a slim wallet carrying all the same stuff is rather clever.”
  1. The Claim: “Our wallets are better designed than traditional wallets to carry all the stuff you have now but be much slimmer.” → To support that claim compare your wallet to the competition side by side. → In other words: You want your value proposition supported with strong reasons to believe.

Value Proposition Canvas

  • Important: Think of a typical customer and design the VPC exclusively for him/ her (can still be a greater group like “Upper middle class with US$100k/ yr income).
  • Shared language in a map to communicate the findings concerning the customers across a team and every part of an organization (tech, marketing, finance, …)
  • Map the customers in an understandable manner for everyone involved in a product/ service.

Observation part of the VPC (observe the customer)/ Customer segment/ Customer profile:

  1. Customer Jobs → Jobs to-be-done of a potential customer → What are the customers’ needs and what do they want in their life/ what’s important to them? → Include different tasks, problems and wishes the customers are intended to deal with. → Redefine the customer segment from the emotional (preferences, popularity), social (reputation, sense of duty), and functional (practicability) perspectives. → Examples (Electric Car - Tesla): personal mobility, occasional long distance trip, commute (pendeln) to work, be different from others, convey the images of success, in synch with personal values
  1. Pains → What are the pains of buyers (i.e. (das heißt) his/ her problems, risks, obstacles, frustrations, …) → Outcome customers want to avoid → Examples (Electric Car - Tesla): lack of charging stations, …)
  1. Gains → What are the gains the customers get when they use the product → Outcome customers want to achieve → Should include the things that make customers satisfied or even happy rather than simply being a converse of the pain. → May be quite existential and free of any extraordinary nature. → Examples (Electric Car - Tesla): performs like a sport car, high-end battery tech, high safety rating, design, brand recognition, self-driving, range of 250-350km, …

  • Once done with the steps below, order the items of each category in importance from highest to lowest.

Design part of the VPC (make sense of the observations)/ Value Proposition segment:

  • How to create value for the customer described in the first part (the whole steps above) of the Value Proposition Canvas? → Result describes the Product Market Fit → Result consists of the choices of what to do and what not to do.
  1. Products and Services → Describe exact products and services you offer for this customer segment. → Make explicit how your products and services connect with the job’s pains and gains of your customers. This part is NOT about the features but about explaining how you create this connection. → How do the products and services create value for the described customers → Examples (Electric Car - Tesla): great battery tech with 60-85 kWh, 8 year battery warranty, various options to add, …
  1. Pain Relievers → How do our products and services kill or relieve the pains from the first part of the VPC? → Only address some of the pains (the most important once) since you cannot address all. → Examples (Electric Car - Tesla): charging 45-90 km/h (addresses topics like frequent charging or the fear of a dead battery), 5+2 seats (addresses the fear of little spacing), range of 350-450km, safest car in the world, …
  1. Gain Creators → How do your P&S produce gains your customers expect, want or desire? → Examples (Electric Car - Tesla): Performance 0-100km/h in 4.4sec, focus on design & style, high-tech 17” touch screen (for the techy feel in the car)

Product-Market Fit

  • Product-Market Fit is a scenario or state of your product in which a company’s target customers are buying, using, and telling others about the company’s product in numbers large enough to sustain that product’s growth and profitability. → The right product is being brought together with the right market. → One of a company’s greatest goals is to reach Product-Market Fit. → Achieving product-market fit means a startup moves out of the learning phase and into understanding its customers and having the first market-ready version of its product. It heralds (einläuten) the scaling phase in which startups take venture capital companies on board to invest into growth.

How to achieve Product-Market Fit:

  1. Determine your target customers
  1. Identify underserved needs of that customers
  1. Define your value proposition
  1. Specify your minimum viable product (MVP) feature set
  1. Develop your MVP
  1. Test your MVP with customers

→ Then, based on those user tests and surveys, use metrics like the ones suggested below, to determine if you’re headed in the right direction.

How to measure the Product-Market Fit:

  • By an experiment: If you ask existing customers how they would feel about it, if they couldn’t use the product anymore and at least 40% say that they would be “very disappointed”, you achieved product-market fit, according to Sean Ellis (marketing expert).
  • Product-Market Fit measurement signals by Andrew Chen:
    • When surveying potential customers or allowing them to test your product, does some segment indicate they will switch to your product?
    • Are some users who have rejected similar products on the market willing to try yours?
    • When user testing, do people group your product accurately with the right competitive offerings?
    • Do users demonstrate an understanding of your product’s differentiators or unique value proposition?
    • How do your underlying metrics (such as retention rates of users) measure up against those of your competitors?

    → To measure product success, you will want to include a mix of both, qualitative and quantitative metrics.

    Quantitative:

    • NPS (net promoter score) score → The likelihood that survey respondents would recommend a company, product, or a service to a friend or colleague.
    • Churn rate (Fluktuationsrate)
    • Growth rate
    • Market share

    Qualitative:

    • Word of mouth → Marc Andreessen: “If your customers talk about your product with others, they effectively become your product’s sales reps.”
    • Calls from the media or industry analysts come in much more frequently, and coverage of your product and company increases.

  • Write different test cards for different idea hypotheses and use them to test and validate your ideas/features with real people. → You ask yourself: What needs to be true for this feature/ pricing model/ etc. to work out?

TAM (Total Addressable Market):

Total sales of all companies in your industry plus potential sales from any qualified customer who hasn’t yet purchased in the category.

Total industry sales / TAM = Market penetration or % of total market reached (lower number is good!)

Total industry sales of [Company X] / Total industry sales = Revenue Market Share (lower number is good! → much upside potential)

TAM Groups:

  1. Qualified customers not buying anything
  1. Current loyal exclusive customers
  1. Customers buying from you and competitor
  1. Customers exclusively buying from competition
Competitors
  • Direct → Sells same product/ service and delivers same benefits
  • Indirect → Sells similar product/ service with different benefits
  • Substitute → Sells unrelated product/ service that can substitute for yours → Ersatzprodukt
SWOT Analysis
  • Strength → Things own company does well (assets, techs, processes, unique capabilities)
  • Weaknesses → Areas that need improvement
  • Opportunities → Favorable situations that can positively effect the business
  • Threats → Favorable situations that can negatively effect the business

Tactical Section (4 Ps)

Describe 4 Ps in detail (Product, Price, Promotion, Place):

  • Product and Service Programs

    → Includes the entire experience of buying an using a product

    • Product and service performance → Product design → Tactical design → Packaging → People involved with customers

    → Start by listing the changes or improvements needed in your product and services. Look at your product service analysis and your positioning statement.

    • Consider Product Support → List any other assets or activities needed that go into supporting the products and services (e.g. installation services for product, repair facilities, customer training programs, customers service desk, etc.)
    • Supplemental Parts or Services → Do you sell accessories or spare parts? → Do you offer a warranty or return policy?

    Entrepreneurial challenges in product policy:

    • Scarce (Slack) resources, can handle only limited complexity
    • "Zero" (or at least little) tolerance for failure in product development since most start-ups are single-product companies -> no cross-subsidizing
    • Product development is often limited to the existing competencies and resources
    • Especially with innovative technologies involved the "right" mix of product/service characteristics is often difficult to determine → compare "strategy as a bottom-up process"

    Remedies for entrepreneurial challenges:

    • Experimentation
    • Early customer integration → Lean Startup logic e. g. MVPs (very basic product with focus on key value), mock sales (present a product as it would already exist and sell it), crowdfunding
    • Creative branding
    • Partnering?

    • AIDA-Modell
      • Awareness → Grab the customers attention and deeply engage with them. Target audience will become curious about the product. → Customer asks: “What is it?” → Increased brand awareness and effective messaging → Example:
        • Content-marketing to attract visitors to your website
      • Interest → Once target audience is aware and gains interest, the goal is to get them to think: “I like it.” → Content must be persuasive and engaging → This stage is about holding the customers’ attention. → Example:
        • “Hook” interested visitors with engaging storytelling that demonstrates the why behind your solution.
        • Convey information in a way that stimulates empathy and curiosity.
      • Desire → Goal is to change “I like it.” to “I want it.” → Cementing trust in brand and product → Keep serving content → Make sure interested people:
        • subscribe to your blog
        • follow you on social media
        • download your offers

        → The more (interested) people interact with your brand, the more they’ll trust you → Examples:

        • Establish social proof with case studies and testimonials
        • “Before and After” style content → stimulate desire, while gaining trust
          • HowTo: Institute a gap (Lücke schaffen/ erstellen/ aufzeigen) between where they are and where they could be with your solution.
            • Example: "Convert 60% more PPC leads into bookings using Black Propeller's secret weapon.” → Helps a prospect envision a future with this product (What would my life be like if I achieve similar results?)

        • … connected to low-friction but high-incentive calls to action. (geringe Reibung, hoher Anreiz)
        • … “high value”. → You must help them in some way.
        • … prominent, clear and uncomplicated. → Buttons and banners that spell out what action they must take and what they get if they do.

        • email offers, printed mailers, broadcast spots, point-of-sale displays
        • Anything that pushes specific advertising outward.
      • Pull marketing: → Also known as inbound marketing → Actively pulling prospects onto your site or drawing them to your product → Focus on maximizing awareness of brand → Pull marketing uses brand awareness and visibility to pull leads to your site → Web-based strategy: You reach out to many customers to attract some who may be interested → You’re convincing customers to seek out to your product on their own rather than directly targeting them. → Examples:
        • SEO, Social Media, cross promotion (collaborating with another existing brand to build brand awareness and trust)

  • Place/ Distribution/ channel design

    Distribution Plan

    A pathway to get your products or services to the customer.

    Three different categories of things that are moved and distributed throughout the distributing process:

    1. Product → Flows from manufacturer/ reseller to the customer (Length, Breath, Depth of channel and different channel partners play a role)
    1. Money → money, credit card balances, financing, negotiations and perhaps contracting
    1. Information → Flows in both directions → from me to the customer and vice versa. → can be information about: product, price, availability, promotional message (value proposition), new product(s)

    Length of channel → The number of intermediaries (Vermittler) between you and the customer.

    Possibilities:

    1. Sell direct → Short channel length
    1. Sell indirect → Longer channel length → Hire partners to ship your product, warehouse and sell your product.

    Breadth of channel → The number of outlets of each type of partner → e. g. exclusive distribution → just a few outlets are selling your products/ services (comparison between “Tiffany & Co.” and “Starbucks” )

    Depth of channel

    → How much of the channel you own and control versus having a third party do it for you.

    Assign Channel Member Roles

    • List actions needed to get product to customer → Packaging → Shipping → Warehouse → Retailing
    • Use the list above to assign roles to the most appropriate channel member → Wholesaler → Retailer → Shipping

Marketing Plan Rollout → Decide communication timeline → List and invite important personnel (Mitarbeiter) → Outline program responsibilities → Show implementation timelines → Include implementation details → Set up KPIs (Key Performance Indicators → help you keep track of your overall strategy and your individual marketing programs) → Each KPI should have a target of what you expect to happen plus a high and low number around that target. You and your planning team should agree in advance what action you’ll take if those thresholds are exceeded (high or low).

Good marketers not only reach their marketing goals, but the also know whether those goals were achieved the way they expected them to be achieved. They also take immediate action when they detect something is going in the wrong direction. A well written marketing plan helps you and your team do just that.

KPI (Key Performance Indicator)

  • A Key Performance Indicator aka KPI is a measurable value that demonstrates how effectively a company is achieving key business objectives. → This can be: number of sales made, revenue made, number of new costumers, … → KPIs shouldn’t spur additional questions, they should do the opposite: inspire action! → KPIs help to estimate how fast you are getting closer to a certain goal even before you reach it and whether you are likely to reach it. Example: So, you set your KPI: sell 1,000 apples this month. Whether that’s 250 apples per week or you sell all 1,000 in the first 3 days, your KPI is to reach that 1K mark. When it’s the second week of September and you’ve sold 550 apples, you can look at your KPI and know that you’re on track to achieve your goal.
  • A good KPI is realistic, straightforward and easy to measure They should be… → … aligned with the overall business strategy and outcomes → … actionable. You need to be able to outline the steps you’ll take to reach the KPI. → realistic. → measurable and include metrics to track your progress against your KPI.
  • Distinction (Unterscheidung) between: → High-level KPIs: They look at the performance of the business as a whole. Example: Increase MRR (monthly recurring revenue) by 20% by the end of the fiscal year → Low-level KPIs: They look at the performance of certain departments, teams or individuals (which contributes to the overall business goal/ KPI) Example: A KPI of a sales team member could be to increase inbound leads by 50% by the end of Q3

What are the advantages/ disadvantages of being the first to market?

  • Advantages:
    • Keine Konkurrenz zu Beginn
    • Preishoheit
    • ‘Die’ Marke (Tempo etc)
    • Einzige Anfrage bei Vertriebspartnern -> Chance mehr Mehrwert mit neuem Produkt
    • Bild der Produktart kann durch die eigene Umsetzung geprägt werden
    • Produktgestaltung -> Art / Design / Menge
    • Dauerhafter Vorteil, dass man immer einen Schritt weiter ist als die Konkurrenz
  • Disadvantages:
    • Keine Bekanntheit für das Produkt
    • Vertriebspartner finden ist schwerer
    • Kein Kennwerte von anderen Unternehmen zum Vergleich
    • Ängste/ Befürchtungen über Nachteile des Produkts sind noch sehr präsent
    • Marke setzt sich nicht durch und Kopie übernimmt Marktanteil
    • Preisanpassung von Konkurrenz -> Unterbietung
    • Konkurrenz Verbessert Produkt
    • Man kann sich an keinen Kennwerten orientieren und der Erfolg des Produkts ist ungewisser als bei etablierten Produkten
    • Noch kein Markt(potenzial)

What parts of the month or week are more suitable for product launches? Why is that so?

  • Beginning of the month → More money (can also be at the end of the month)
  • might depend on the product
  • End of the week so customers have more time to interact with the product
  • Monday less consume (B2B & B2C → orders etc. from weekday have to be handled first) & Friday also less consume since many people are already in the weekend with their head (except for events, trades, etc. that go over the weekend).
  • Tuesdays, Wednesdays and Thursdays make more sense for many product launches.
  • Depending on the niche, there can be a lot of sales at the end of the year because many companies have to spend a certain budget by the end of the year (reasons: tax reasons or because some parts of a company have certain budgets that they haven't fully spent yet but want to spend in order to maintain the same amount next year - and no one says, "Looks like you don't need that much, we'll cut your budget").
